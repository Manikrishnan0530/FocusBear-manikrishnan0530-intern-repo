## Research & Learning
## What AI tools are typically used for this role?
In most knowledge-work and software/product roles, common AI tools include:

Writing assistants (e.g., Claude, ChatGPT, Grammarly) — drafting emails, documentation, and reports
Coding assistants (e.g., GitHub Copilot, Claude Code, chatGPT, Gemini) — writing, reviewing, and debugging code
Research tools — summarising long documents, synthesising information quickly
Productivity tools — meeting transcription (e.g., Otter.ai), task automation, and scheduling

## Benefits of using AI in a professional setting

Speeds up repetitive or first-draft tasks (writing, summarising, formatting)
Helps overcome blank-page paralysis and writer's block
Useful for brainstorming and exploring multiple angles on a problem
Can surface relevant information faster than manual research

## Risks of using AI in a professional setting

Data privacy leaks — pasting confidential information into external AI tools can expose it to third parties
Hallucinations — AI can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect facts, figures, or citations
Over-reliance — using AI without critical thinking can lead to poor-quality outputs going unreviewed
Bias and tone issues — AI-generated content may not reflect company voice, cultural context, or accuracy
Intellectual property concerns — AI outputs may inadvertently reproduce copyrighted material

## Information that should NEVER be entered into AI tools

Personal data about users, customers, or colleagues (names, emails, health info)
Confidential business strategies, financials, or unreleased product plans
Login credentials, API keys, or security tokens
Private communications (e.g., internal Slack threads or sensitive HR matters)
Any information covered by an NDA or confidentiality agreement

## How to fact-check and validate AI-generated content

Cross-reference key claims with primary or authoritative sources (official docs, research papers, trusted websites)
Check specific figures and statistics — AI frequently gets numbers wrong
Verify names, dates, and attributions independently
Read critically — ask yourself whether the output actually makes sense in context
Use your domain knowledge — if something feels off, investigate it


## Reflections
## When should I use AI, and when should I rely on my own skills?
Use AI for:

First drafts of documents, emails, or documentation where structure matters more than originality
Summarising long texts I need to understand quickly
Generating options or ideas to react to (not to copy directly)
Repetitive formatting tasks

Rely on my own skills for:

Final judgment on quality, tone, and accuracy
Decisions that require contextual understanding of the team or product
Anything involving sensitive information
Creative work where my own perspective adds unique value

## How to avoid over-reliance while still benefiting from AI

Treat AI as a first draft partner, not a final author — always review and edit the output
Set a personal rule: if I can write something in under 5 minutes, I write it myself first
Regularly reflect on whether AI is making me more capable or just faster at bypassing thinking
Use AI to learn (e.g., ask it to explain something), not just to produce

## Steps I will take to ensure data privacy when using AI tools

Never paste personal, confidential, or user-identifying information into external AI tools
Check whether a tool stores or trains on my inputs (and opt out where possible)
Use internally approved or privacy-safe AI tools for work tasks involving sensitive content
When in doubt, anonymise or generalise before prompting (e.g., replace names with "User A")
