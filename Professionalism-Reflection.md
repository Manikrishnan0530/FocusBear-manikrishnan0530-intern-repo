# Professionalism at Focus Bear — Reflection

## Research & Learning

### Professional vs. Unprofessional Behaviour in a Casual Remote Team

At Focus Bear, professionalism isn't about wearing a suit or using formal language — it's about being someone your teammates can count on. The distinction comes down to respect and accountability:

- **Being reliable** — saying what you'll do and actually doing it
- **Communicating clearly and on time** — even in casual channels like Discord
- **Showing up prepared** — bringing questions and context to meetings, not winging it

The common thread is that professionalism is about how your behaviour affects others around you. You can be relaxed, funny, and yourself — and still be deeply professional. Those things aren't in conflict.

### Respectful Communication in Remote Teams

Text-based communication strips away tone of voice and body language, which means it's very easy to come across as cold, dismissive, or blunt without meaning to. Respectful remote communication requires extra intentionality:

- **Assume good intent first** — before reacting to a message that stings, ask a clarifying question rather than assuming the worst
- **Keep your tone warm** — a short message can land harshly; a little care in wording goes a long way
- **Focus on the work, not the person** — when there's disagreement, anchor the conversation to the task or outcome, not the individual

Remote teams run on trust, and trust is built one interaction at a time. Every message is an opportunity to reinforce or erode it.

### Giving and Receiving Feedback Professionally

Feedback is one of the most valuable things that can happen in a professional relationship — but only if both sides approach it well. Good feedback culture at Focus Bear means:

- **Being specific about the action, never the person** — "This section of the report needs more clarity" lands very differently to "You didn't explain this well"
- **Asking for clarification before assuming negative intent** — if feedback feels harsh, ask what the person meant before reacting defensively
- **Receiving it with openness** — feedback is a gift, even when it's uncomfortable. The goal is growth, not judgment

Giving and receiving feedback well isn't a personality trait — it's a skill you build deliberately, through practice and genuine self-awareness.

### Key Takeaways from the Supervisor Guide

After reading the [How to Work with Supervisors at Focus Bear](https://www.canva.com/design/DAGV81P68ac/2EjPdtpfnUBbIQjDbn9XzQ/edit) guide, the overarching message was clear: **be proactive and don't wait to be managed**. Specific things that stood out:

- **Come prepared to every meeting** — read the brief, know your progress, bring real questions
- **Take notes and follow up on action items** — covering the same ground week after week wastes everyone's time
- **Escalate communication based on urgency** — email → Discord → SMS → call. Most things aren't urgent, so start light and escalate deliberately
- **Don't ghost your project** — even a quick "still working on it" keeps everyone in sync and builds trust

The guide made it clear that supervisors at Focus Bear genuinely want interns to succeed — but the intern has to meet them halfway by taking ownership of their own experience.

## Reflections

### Great Teamwork — What Made It Work

The best teamwork I've experienced had a few things in common: everyone knew their role and trusted each other to deliver, communication was open enough that people flagged blockers early instead of quietly struggling, and there was genuine psychological safety — no one feared asking questions or admitting when something wasn't going well.

What made it effective wasn't talent alone — it was the combination of accountability and trust. People held themselves to a high standard *and* created space for others to do the same without fear.

### Making My Communication Clear, Respectful, and Inclusive

Inclusive communication doesn't happen by accident — it's something I need to be deliberate about. My commitments at Focus Bear:

- **Be clear and concise** — say what I mean without leaving room for misinterpretation
- **Check my tone before hitting send** — especially in async text, where warmth doesn't come automatically
- **Use inclusive language** — be mindful that teammates have different communication styles, backgrounds, and needs

Every message I send shapes how people experience working with me. I want that experience to be easy, not effortful.

### Creating a Positive, Fun, and Professional Environment

The two things aren't mutually exclusive. I can contribute to a great culture by showing up with energy and good humour, celebrating teammates' wins, being the kind of person others feel comfortable asking questions of, and keeping things light without ever being dismissive or careless. Culture is built from the small interactions, not the big moments.

### Preparing Effectively for Meetings

Before any meeting with a supervisor or colleague, I will:

1. **Read any relevant briefs or documents beforehand** — never show up cold
2. **Know where I am on my tasks** — progress, blockers, and next steps
3. **Prepare specific questions** — not vague ones I could have Googled
4. **Have a short agenda** — even a rough one shows respect for everyone's time

The supervisor guide made it clear: don't wait for the supervisor to lead the whole thing. Come ready to dive in.

### Being Proactive During My Internship

Being proactive means owning my internship experience rather than waiting for it to happen to me. In practice that looks like:

- Identifying what needs doing and proposing it — not sitting idle waiting for instructions
- Sending end-of-day updates without being asked, so my supervisor is never in the dark
- Coming to every meeting with an agenda and questions already prepared
- If I'm stuck: searching online or asking ChatGPT first → then a teammate → then my supervisor if still blocked after 30 minutes

The guide put it well: *"Don't wait to be told what to do but also don't go rogue."* The sweet spot is forming a view on what to do next, checking in to confirm, and then getting on with it.

### Following Up and Escalating

My approach to following up when I need a response:

1. **Send the initial message on the appropriate channel** based on urgency — email for low priority, Discord for medium, SMS for urgent
2. **Follow up once politely** if I don't hear back within the expected timeframe — a nudge is always welcome, according to the guide
3. **Escalate to the next channel** if still no response — not out of frustration, but because sometimes things get missed and a different channel is the right tool
4. **Call only for genuine emergencies** — things that truly can't wait

The key is not letting silence stall important work. One polite follow-up is professional. Waiting indefinitely without escalating isn't.

### What I Will NOT Do at Focus Bear

Drawing directly from the supervisor guide, I commit to avoiding:

-  **Ghosting my project** — no updates is not an option, even "still working on it" is better than silence
-  **Coming to meetings unprepared** — winging it wastes everyone's time, including mine
-  **Not taking notes** — and definitely not asking the same questions twice because I wasn't paying attention
-  **Asking questions I could Google in 10 seconds** — I'll do the basic research first and save supervisor time for the harder stuff
-  **Taking feedback personally or repeating the same mistakes** — if the same feedback keeps coming up, something isn't being heard
-  **Relying on only one communication channel when things are urgent** — I'll escalate when it matters
-  **Sending vague calendar invites** — every invite will include the project name and a short agenda

### CEO Contact for Major Conduct Issues

As noted in the onboarding deck, the CEO's phone number has been saved for use in cases of **major breaches of professional conduct or serious unresolved issues**. This is a last-resort escalation path — to be used when normal channels have failed and the issue is significant enough to warrant direct contact.

Day-to-day concerns should always be handled through the normal escalation ladder: teammate → supervisor → escalate further as needed. The CEO contact is reserved for situations where that process has broken down or the issue is of a serious enough nature to bypass it.
